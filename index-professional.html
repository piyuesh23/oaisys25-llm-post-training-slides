Myth #1

Fine-Tuning = Adding Knowledge

"If I fine-tune ChatGPT on our Q4 budget, it'll know all the numbers!"

The Reality

Fine-tuning teaches behavior, tone, and patterns — not facts.

Think of it like teaching your dog a new trick. You're not uploading "knowledge" into its brain, you're reinforcing response patterns.

The Solution

Need the model to know specific facts?

Use RAG to give it access to the actual documents. Otherwise you're training it to sound confident about numbers it's making up.

Myth #2

More Data = Better Model

The Assumption

"Let's throw ALL our data at the model. More is better, right?"

The Reality

More bad data = more bad outputs. Quality beats quantity every single time.

The Truth

Curate your training data like you're Marie Kondo-ing your closet — does this data spark actual value?

Key Insight: Fine-tuning on messy, contradictory, or irrelevant data is like studying for a test by reading the entire internet. You'll learn chaos.

Myth #3

RAG is Basically Cheating

"Using RAG means the model isn't really learning!"

Hot Take: RAG Isn't Cheating

Retrieval-Augmented Generation gives the model access to external knowledge at runtime. It's like letting someone use a calculator during a math test — it's the right tool for the job.

Use RAG When:

  • Your data changes frequently
  • You need citations and sources
  • The information wasn't in the training data
  • You want to avoid hallucinations
Myth #4

You Can Train Away Hallucinations

The Hard Truth

Hallucinations — those moments when ChatGPT invents facts with the confidence of a game show host — are baked into how LLMs work.

They're next-token prediction machines, not truth oracles. Fine-tuning won't magically fix this.

Myth #5

Models Understand Your Vague Vibes

What You Said

"Just make it sound more... professional?"

What Model Heard

"Execute undefined_vibe.exe... ERROR 404"

LLMs Need Specificity

LLMs are pattern-matching machines. They need clear, specific instructions.

Instead of hoping fine-tuning will telepathically understand your brand voice, give concrete examples. Show, don't tell.

Pro Tip: If your prompt is vague, your output will be vague. Garbage in, artisanal garbage out.

Real-World Example

Customer Support Bot Transformation

❌ The Wrong Approach

Strategy: Fine-tune on all historical tickets

  • Cost: $5,000 upfront
  • Time: 3 weeks
  • Result: Outdated info, hallucinated policies
  • Accuracy: 65%

✓ The Right Approach

Strategy: RAG + Prompt Engineering

  • Cost: $500/month
  • Time: 3 days
  • Result: Always current, cites sources
  • Accuracy: 92%

The Lesson: Fine-tuning taught the bot HOW to respond, but RAG gave it WHAT to say. The combination of better prompting + RAG delivered 10x faster at 1/10th the cost.

ML Glossary for Normal Humans

Essential terminology decoded

Model

The "brain" created after training. It's basically a mathematical pattern-matcher.

Tokens

Breaking text into crumbs so the model can digest it. "ChatGPT" becomes ["Chat", "G", "PT"].

Embeddings

The model's secret love language. Words get converted into numbers that capture meaning.

Weights

The billions of tiny knobs inside the model that get adjusted during training.

Attention

The part of the model that decides which words matter most in a sentence.

Training

Feeding the model lots of data so it can learn patterns. Like teaching a dog tricks, but with math.

ML Glossary (Continued)

Inference

Using the trained model to answer questions or make decisions. The model's "showtime."

Labels

The answer the model is supposed to learn. The actual house price in training data.

Overfitting

When the model memorizes training data instead of learning to generalize.

Underfitting

When the model hasn't learned enough — too simple to capture useful patterns.

Parameters

The "knobs" inside the model that get tuned during training. (Similar to weights but more general)

Bias

A systematic mistake. Model favors one type of outcome unfairly.

How LLMs Actually Work

The 1000-Foot View

Next-Token Prediction

LLMs predict the next most likely token. They don't have a "truth database" — they're sophisticated autocomplete on steroids.

Sometimes the most likely next word is confidently wrong.

Training Gaps

The model never saw certain information, so it interpolates based on patterns.

"Like a student who didn't study for a test and is winging the essay question with 'vibes.'"

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Learn from others' expensive mistakes

1

Training Without Baseline

Starting fine-tuning without measuring current performance. You won't know if you improved or made things worse.

2

Mixing Knowledge & Behavior

Trying to teach facts AND tone in the same fine-tuning run. Use RAG for facts, fine-tuning for style.

3

Ignoring Data Quality

Throwing all available data at the model. 100 clean examples beat 10,000 messy ones every time.

4

Over-Optimizing Test Sets

Tuning until your test metrics are perfect. You've memorized the test, not learned the task.

5

Skipping Prompt Engineering

Jumping straight to fine-tuning. Try better prompts first — it's free and takes minutes, not weeks.

When NOT to Post-Train

Red Flags That Should Make You Stop

It's Actually a UX Problem

Users are confused because your interface is bad, not because the model is bad. Fix the UI first.

You Have <50 Quality Examples

Fine-tuning needs hundreds of good examples minimum. With less, you're just teaching it to overfit.

You Haven't Tried Better Prompts

90% of "we need fine-tuning" problems can be solved with better prompt engineering. Start there.

Chasing Benchmarks, Not Value

Optimizing for leaderboard scores instead of actual business metrics. Your users don't care about BLEU scores.

Your Data is Messy/Contradictory

Training on inconsistent data teaches inconsistent behavior. Clean your data or don't train at all.

No Clear Success Criteria

If you can't define what "better" means with metrics, you can't measure if training worked.
